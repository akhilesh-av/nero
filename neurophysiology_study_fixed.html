Section 01

Definition of Local Anesthesia

Write this definition verbatim in any long-answer question about local anesthetics.

Standard Definition (Covino & Vassallo, 1976)
Local anesthesia is defined as loss of sensation in a circumscribed area of the body caused by depression of excitation in nerve endings or inhibition of the conduction process in peripheral nerves. An important feature is that it produces this loss of sensation without inducing loss of consciousness.
⚡
Key exam point: Local anesthesia ≠ General anesthesia — the single most important distinguishing feature is that consciousness is preserved. This must be stated in any definition-based question.

Methods to Induce Local Anesthesia (6 Methods)

Enumerate in order
1. Mechanical trauma (compression of tissues)
2. Low temperature
3. Anoxia
4. Chemical irritants
5. Neurolytic agents — alcohol and phenol
6. Chemical agents — local anesthetics

Only methods that induce a transient and completely reversible state of anesthesia have clinical application.
Section 02

Desirable Properties of Local Anesthetics High Yield

Exam frequently asks: "Enumerate and discuss the ideal properties of a local anesthetic."

⚠️
Exam Tip: There are two lists — 6 properties (standard) and 4 additional properties (Bennett). Learn both sets and which ones most clinical agents fail to meet.

Primary 6 Desirable Properties

1. Non-irritating to tissues
Must not cause local tissue damage at site of injection. Most clinical agents satisfy this.
2. No permanent nerve damage
Effects must be completely reversible. No permanent alteration of nerve structure. Most agents satisfy this.
3. Low systemic toxicity Paramount
All injectable + most topical agents are absorbed into the cardiovascular system. Toxicity varies greatly among agents. Most important property.
4. Effective by injection AND topically
Not all agents meet this. Procaine, mepivacaine — poor topical agents. Lidocaine, tetracaine — effective both ways. Dyclonine = potent topical, NOT injectable (tissue-irritating).
5. Rapid onset
Time from injection to complete blockade should be minimal. Related to pKa of the drug. Most clinical agents meet this.
6. Adequate duration
Long enough to complete the procedure; not so long as to require extended recovery. Varies considerably among drugs and injection types.

4 Additional Properties (Bennett)

Sufficient potency
Complete anesthesia without harmful concentrated solutions.
Non-allergenic
Relatively free from producing allergic reactions. Esters carry higher allergy risk.
Stable + undergoes biotransformation
Stable in solution; readily metabolised in the body.
Sterilizable by heat
Without deterioration.
📌
No local anesthetic in current use satisfies ALL desirable criteria. However, all meet most of them. Research continues to maximize desirable factors and minimize negative ones.
Section 03

The Neuron — Structure

Neuron anatomy forms the basis for understanding where and how local anesthetics act.

Core Principle
Local anesthetics prevent both the generation AND conduction of a nerve impulse. They set up a chemical roadblock between the pain source and brain. The aborted impulse, prevented from reaching the brain, cannot be interpreted as pain.
Sensory (Afferent) Neuron — 3 Parts
  • Peripheral process (dendritic zone): Free nerve endings — most distal; detect stimuli
  • Axon: Long cable; transmits impulse centrally to CNS
  • Cell body: Located away from axon; provides metabolic support ONLY — NOT involved in impulse transmission
Motor (Efferent) Neuron
  • Cell body is interposed between axon and dendrites
  • Cell body is integral to impulse transmission (unlike sensory)
  • Axon branches near termination → bulbous axon terminals (boutons)
  • Boutons synapse with skeletal muscle at motor end plate

The Axon

Structure
Long cylinder of neural cytoplasm (axoplasm) enclosed in thin sheath — the nerve membrane (axolemma). Axoplasm separated from extracellular fluid by a continuous nerve membrane.
Importance
Both sensory nerve excitability and conduction are attributable to changes in the nerve membrane. Cell body and axoplasm are NOT essential for conduction — they provide metabolic support for the membrane.
Myelin
Some nerves covered by insulating lipid-rich myelin layer. Enables myelinated nerves to conduct at much faster rates than unmyelinated nerves of equal size. Myelin = 75% lipid, 20% protein, 5% carbohydrate.
Section 04

Nerve Membrane — Structure & Channels Exam Favourite

Detailed membrane structure is commonly asked in short answer and essay questions.

Membrane Structure
• Thickness: 70–80 Å (1 Å = 1/10,000 of a micrometer)
• Flexible, nonstretchable structure
• Two layers of lipid molecules = bilipid layer of phospholipids
• Lipids oriented with hydrophilic (polar) ends facing outward and hydrophobic (nonpolar) tails facing inward (middle)
• Proteins are primary organizational elements: classified as transport proteins (channels, carriers, pumps) and receptor sites
Channel Proteins
Continuous pores through the membrane. Allow some ions (Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺) to flow passively. Some channels are gated — permit ion flow only when gate is open. Na⁺ channel internal diameter: 0.3–0.5 nm. Na⁺ passes through 12 times more easily than K⁺.
Resting Membrane Resistance
Resting nerve membrane has electrical resistance ~50 times greater than intracellular and extracellular fluids. Prevents Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻ from passing down their gradients. During impulse, conductivity increases approximately 100-fold.
Hydrated Ion Sizes — Why Na⁺ Can't Enter at Rest
All ions attract water → become hydrated.
Hydrated Na⁺ radius = 3.4 Å
Hydrated K⁺ and Cl⁻ radius = 2.2 Å
Na⁺ is ~50% larger. Too large to pass through narrow resting channels. K⁺ and Cl⁻ can pass freely.
Sodium Channel Gates
Sodium channel gates are located near the external surface of the nerve membrane. Potassium channel gates are located near the internal surface. This asymmetry is clinically significant for drug binding.

Action Potential — Electrophysiology High Yield

Must know all values, stages, and timing for short answer and MCQ questions.

Action Potential — Four Phases with Exact Values
Resting State
−70 mV
Interior negative. Membrane impermeable to Na⁺ at rest.
Step 1A–B: Slow Depol.
−50 to −60 mV
Na⁺ channels begin opening. Threshold = −55 mV. Need ↓15 mV.
Step 1C: Rapid Depol.
+40 mV
Massive Na⁺ influx. Interior becomes POSITIVE. 0.3 ms duration.
Step 2: Repolarization
−70 mV
Na⁺ permeability inactivated. K⁺ efflux. Returns to −70 mV. 0.7 ms.
📝
Exact values to write in exam: Resting = −70 mV | Firing threshold = −50 to −60 mV | Voltage change needed = 15 mV | Peak depolarization = +40 mV | Total cycle = 1 ms (Depol = 0.3 ms, Repol = 0.7 ms)

Sodium Channel States — C, O, I MCQ Favourite

  • C
    Closed State — Resting
    Nonconducting. Channels are in this state when membrane is at −70 mV. Voltage-responsive — can be recruited to Open state by depolarization. This is the normal resting configuration.
  • O
    Open State — Depolarization
    Ion-conducting state. Na⁺ rapidly enters axoplasm. Occurs during depolarization. Configurational changes produce transient widening of transmembrane channels to allow hydrated Na⁺ to pass (C → O transformation).
  • I
    Inactive State — Absolute Refractory Period
    Nonconducting. Key difference from C state: depolarization CANNOT recruit channels from I state to O state. Only C → O is possible. Channels are "locked" in this state temporarily. This is the basis of the absolute refractory period. Slowly converts back to C state with repolarization.

Refractory Periods

Absolute Refractory Period
Immediately after stimulus. Nerve is unable to respond to any stimulus regardless of strength. Corresponds to the I (inactive) state of Na⁺ channels. Lasts for about the duration of the main part of the action potential.
Relative Refractory Period
Follows absolute refractory period. A new impulse can be initiated, but only by a stronger-than-normal stimulus. Continues to decrease until normal excitability returns — at which point the nerve is said to be repolarized.

Sodium Pump — Active Transport

After repolarization
After return to −70 mV, slight excess Na⁺ exists intracellularly and slight excess K⁺ extracellularly. Active transport then occurs:
• Na⁺ pumped OUT of cell against concentration gradient
• K⁺ pumped IN against concentration gradient
• Energy source: oxidative metabolism of adenosine triphosphate (ATP)
• Movement of Na⁺ during depolarization and K⁺ during repolarization is passive (down concentration gradients — no energy needed)
• Process of repolarization requires 0.7 milliseconds

Behaviour of Each Ion at Rest

K⁺ — Stays Inside Despite Gradient
Membrane freely permeable to K⁺. Concentration gradient favours efflux (high inside). BUT the negative charge of the membrane restrains the positively charged K⁺ by electrostatic attraction. Net result: K⁺ remains inside.
Cl⁻ — Stays Outside Despite Gradient
Membrane freely permeable to Cl⁻. Concentration gradient favours influx (high outside). BUT an opposing nearly equal electrostatic gradient forces outward migration. Net result: no diffusion of Cl⁻ through membrane.
Na⁺ — Blocked by Impermeability
Both concentration gradient (greater outside) AND electrostatic gradient (positive ion attracted inward) favour influx. Only relative impermeability of the resting membrane to Na⁺ prevents massive influx. Hydrated Na⁺ (3.4 Å) > channel width at rest.
🔬
Safety factor for conduction: 5–6 times the minimum Na⁺ permeability required. Local anesthetics selectively inhibit peak Na⁺ permeability, reducing this safety factor. When safety factor falls below unity (1.0), conduction fails and nerve block occurs.
Section 07

Impulse Propagation & Saltatory Conduction Essay Question

Commonly asked as "describe the spread of impulse in myelinated vs unmyelinated nerves."

Unmyelinated Nerve Conduction
  • Impulse spreads by sequential depolarization of short adjoining membrane segments
  • Characterized as a slow forward-creeping process
  • Conduction rate: C fibres = 1.2 m/s
  • High-resistance membrane + low-resistance axoplasm/extracellular fluid = rapid current density decrease with distance
  • Local current only adequate to depolarize immediately adjacent membrane
Myelinated Nerve — Saltatory Conduction
  • Saltare = Latin for "to leap"
  • Current leaps from node of Ranvier to node of Ranvier
  • Much faster and more energy efficient
  • Conduction rate: Aα and Aδ = 14.8–120 m/s
  • Thicker axon = greater distance between nodes = more rapid saltatory conduction
  • Impulse can skip 1–2 blocked nodes — therefore minimum 8–10 mm of nerve must be blocked

Backward Propagation — Why It Doesn't Happen

Unidirectional Propagation
After a segment depolarizes, it enters the absolute and relative refractory periods. Backward (retrograde) movement of the wave of depolarization is prevented by the unexcitable, refractory segment behind the impulse. The wave therefore travels only in one direction.

Mechanism of Action — How LAs Block Nerve Conduction High Yield

The 8-step proposed mechanism must be memorized for long-answer questions.

Ways Local Anesthetics Interfere with Excitation
1. Altering the basic resting potential of the nerve membrane
2. Altering the threshold potential (firing level)
3. Decreasing the rate of depolarization
4. Prolonging the rate of repolarization

Established fact: Primary effects occur during the depolarization phase. Main effect = decrease in rate of depolarization (especially slow depolarization phase). No accompanying change in the rate of repolarization. The resting potential is unaltered by local anesthetics (they do not hyperpolarize).
8-Step Proposed Mechanism of Action (Covino & Vassallo)
01Displacement of Ca²⁺ ions from the sodium channel receptor site — Ca²⁺ normally exerts a regulatory effect on Na⁺ movement; its release is the first step in depolarization
02Binding of the local anesthetic molecule to this receptor site on the sodium channel — RNH⁺ enters from the axoplasmic (internal) side
03Blockade of the sodium channel — channel rendered impermeable (plugged state)
04Decrease in sodium conductance through the nerve membrane
05Depression of the rate of electrical depolarization — particularly the slow depolarization phase
06Failure to achieve the threshold potential level — the −15 mV reduction from resting potential cannot be achieved
07Lack of development of propagated action potentials — local currents do not develop; the membrane's electrical potential remains unchanged
08Conduction blockade — this is termed a nondepolarizing nerve block
📌
Nondepolarizing nerve block — the membrane remains in a polarized state because the ionic movements responsible for the action potential fail to develop. The self-perpetuating mechanism of impulse propagation is stalled.
Section 09

Theories of Mechanism of Action Essay Favourite

"Discuss the theories of mechanism of action of local anesthetics" — classic essay question. Know all theories, their basis, and their limitations.

⚠️
Exam Tip: Know which theories are outdated/discredited and why. Know which is the most accepted. Specific receptor theory = most favoured today.
  • 1
    Acetylcholine Theory — DISCREDITED
    States acetylcholine (ACh) is involved in nerve conduction along the axon body (in addition to its role as neurotransmitter at synapses). Limitation: No evidence exists that ACh is involved in neural transmission along the body of the neuron. Discredited.
  • 2
    Calcium Displacement Theory — LARGELY DISCREDITED
    Local anesthetic nerve block produced by displacement of Ca²⁺ from a membrane site that controls Na⁺ permeability. Was once popular. Limitation: Varying the concentration of calcium ions bathing a nerve does NOT affect local anesthetic potency — this evidence diminished its credibility.
  • 3
    Surface Charge (Repulsion) Theory — LIMITED
    Cationic (RNH⁺) drug molecules align at the membrane–water interface. Their net positive charge makes the membrane surface more positive → decreases nerve excitability by increasing threshold potential. Limitation: Cannot explain activity of uncharged molecules (e.g., benzocaine). Resting potential remains unaltered by LAs — does not support this theory. Conventional LAs act within channels, not at surface.
  • 4
    Membrane Expansion Theory — PARTIALLY ACCEPTED
    Highly lipid-soluble LA molecules diffuse to hydrophobic regions of excitable membranes, producing general disturbance of bulk membrane structure → expanding critical membrane regions → decreased diameter of sodium channels → inhibition of Na⁺ conductance and neural excitation. Explains benzocaine activity. Nerve membranes do expand when exposed to LAs. Limitation: No direct evidence that conduction is entirely blocked by membrane expansion alone.
  • 5
    Specific Receptor Theory — MOST ACCEPTED ✓
    Local anesthetics act by binding to specific receptors on the sodium channel. Action is direct — not mediated by changes in general membrane properties. Both biochemical and electrophysiological studies confirm a specific receptor site (R-LA) in the sodium channel either on its external surface or internal axoplasmic surface. Once LA gains access to receptors, Na⁺ permeability is decreased or eliminated → nerve conduction interrupted. ~90% blocking effect via cationic form (RNH⁺); ~10% via base form (RN) — accounts for Class D drugs.

Chemical Classification: Esters vs. Amides

Amino Esters — Linkage: -COO-
  • Biotransformation: Hydrolysed rapidly in plasma by cholinesterase (aqueous solution)
  • Procaine hydrolysed in plasma in a few minutes
  • Allergy risk: Higher — metabolite is PABA (para-aminobenzoic acid)
  • Excretion: Smaller % excreted unchanged in urine
  • Agents: Procaine, tetracaine, chloroprocaine, benzocaine, cocaine, propoxycaine
  • Mnemonic: "PCAT-B" — Procaine, Chloroprocaine, Articaine, Tetracaine, Benzocaine (but articaine is amide!)
Amino Amides — Linkage: -NHCO-
  • Biotransformation: Relatively resistant to hydrolysis; metabolised in liver
  • ~10% of procainamide hydrolysed in 1 day (vs. procaine in minutes)
  • Allergy risk: Lower
  • Excretion: Greater % excreted unchanged in urine
  • Agents: Lidocaine, mepivacaine, prilocaine, bupivacaine, articaine, ropivacaine, etidocaine
  • Mnemonic: All amides have double "i": LIdocaIne, MepIvacaIne, BupIvacaIne, PrIlocaIne
Typical Local Anesthetic Structure — 3 Parts
1. Lipophilic part (largest portion) — aromatic in structure; derived from benzoic acid, aniline, or thiophene (articaine only). Determines lipid solubility and potency.
2. Intermediate hydrocarbon chain — contains either an ester or amide linkage. Determines ester/amide classification.
3. Hydrophilic part — amino derivative of ethyl alcohol or acetic acid. Determines water solubility and ability to be injected.

All local anesthetics are amphipathic — possess both lipophilic and hydrophilic characteristics at opposite ends of the molecule.

Drugs without a hydrophilic part (e.g., benzocaine) — not suitable for injection but excellent topical anesthetics.
Section 11

pKa, Dissociation & pH Effects High Yield

"Explain the role of pH in local anesthetic activity" — very common exam question including in endodontics context.

Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation
pH = pKa + log [conjugate base (RN)] / [acid (RNH⁺)]

In solution: RNH⁺ ⇌ RN + H⁺

• When pH = pKa: exactly 50% RNH⁺ and 50% RN
• Low pH (acidic) → equilibrium shifts LEFT → more RNH⁺ (cation; hydrophilic; cannot cross membrane)
• High pH (alkaline) → equilibrium shifts RIGHT → more RN (free base; lipophilic; diffuses through sheath)

pKa = dissociation constant = measure of a molecule's affinity for H⁺. Lower pKa = more RN at tissue pH 7.4 = faster onset.
📝
Two-factor action: (1) RN (free base) — lipid-soluble, diffuses through nerve sheath. (2) RNH⁺ (cation) — binds receptor site inside sodium channel from axoplasmic side. Both forms are needed. Diffusibility through sheath is the RATE-LIMITING STEP clinically.

Effect of Inflammation on Local Anesthetic Activity Endodontics Context

Why LAs Fail in Inflamed/Infected Tissue
• Normal tissue pH: 7.4 → ~25% molecules as RN (e.g., for drug with pKa 7.9 → 250 RN per 1000 molecules can cross nerve sheath)
• Inflamed tissue pH: 5–6 → ~99% molecules as RNH⁺ → only ~1% as RN (~10 RN per 1000 molecules cross sheath)
• Additionally: Increased vascularity in inflamed region → accelerated vascular absorption → drug removed from site faster
• Result: Inadequate concentration of LA reaches nerve → anesthesia fails
• Clinically most common in endodontics (pulpitis, periapical abscess)

pH of Commercial LA Solutions

Plain solutions (no vasoconstrictor)
pH range: 5.5–7. When injected, vast buffering capacity of tissues returns pH to 7.4. Plain lidocaine measured at pH 6.00 ± 0.27.
Epinephrine-containing solutions
pH range: 3.5–4.4 (or as low as 2.8–5.5 for dental cartridges). Acidified by sodium bisulfite (0.05–0.1%) to prevent oxidation of epinephrine. 2% lidocaine with epi measured at pH 3.93 ± 0.43.
Clinical effects of low pH solution
• Burning sensation on injection
• Slightly slower onset (more time needed for tissue buffering to raise pH)
• More postinjection soreness at site
Buffering (Alkalinisation)
Adding sodium bicarbonate or CO₂ before injection: elevates pH → more RN available → faster onset, greater comfort. However, LA base precipitates out of alkalinized solutions making storage impossible — must be done immediately before use.

EMLA and Topical Anesthesia

EMLA — Eutectic Mixture of Local Anesthetics
• Contains: lidocaine + prilocaine (eutectic mixture)
• Intact skin is normally impenetrable to topical LAs
• EMLA achieves penetration through intact skin, albeit slowly
• Mucous membranes and injured skin (burns, abrasions) lack intact skin protection → topical LAs work here
• Topical agents manufactured at higher concentrations (5–10% lidocaine) vs injectable (2% lidocaine) to provide adequate RN molecules despite low % at mucosal pH
• Benzocaine is not ionized in solution → effectiveness unaffected by pH → used widely as topical (poor water solubility = minimal systemic absorption)
Section 12

Active Forms & Actions on Nerve Membrane Essay

Detailed mechanism of how RN and RNH⁺ work — step-by-step answer for "how does a local anesthetic block a nerve?"

Why LAs Are Formulated as Acid Salts
As prepared in lab: local anesthetics are basic compounds — poorly water-soluble, unstable on air exposure. pKa values range 7.5–10. Limited clinical value in pure form.

Being weakly basic, they combine with acids to form local anesthetic salts — water-soluble and comparatively stable.

Dispensed as acid salts (most commonly hydrochloride salt) dissolved in sterile water or saline — e.g., lidocaine hydrochloride, articaine hydrochloride.

Step-by-Step: How the Molecule Works (Exam Answer Format)

  • 1
    Drug injected extracellularly (tissue pH 7.4)
    Example: drug with pKa 7.9. At pH 7.4: 75% exists as RNH⁺ (charged cation) and 25% exists as RN (free base). From 1000 molecules → 250 RN molecules available.
  • 2
    RN (free base) diffuses through nerve sheath
    The lipid-soluble, uncharged RN form diffuses through the lipid nerve sheath (axolemma). The charged RNH⁺ cannot cross the lipid barrier. All 250 RN molecules theoretically enter the axoplasm.
  • 3
    Extracellular equilibrium re-established
    Loss of RN disrupts RNH⁺ ⇌ RN equilibrium extracellularly. Remaining 750 RNH⁺ re-equilibrate: 570 RNH⁺ + 180 RN. These 180 new RN molecules also diffuse inward. Process continues repeatedly.
  • 4
    Re-equilibration inside the axoplasm
    Inside nerve, intracellular pH is also 7.4. RN cannot remain entirely as RN inside. 75% of RN converts back to RNH⁺; 25% remains as RN. This conversion to RNH⁺ inside the cell is essential for activity.
  • 5
    RNH⁺ binds receptor in sodium channel
    From the axoplasmic (internal) side, RNH⁺ ions enter the sodium channel and bind to the R-LA receptor site. The channel filter precludes direct entry from the external (extracellular) mouth. This produces conduction blockade.
💡
In practice: not all molecules reach the nerve interior because (1) diffusion is three-dimensional — not just toward the nerve; (2) some absorbed into capillaries and extracellular soft tissue at injection site; (3) ester anesthetics undergo enzymatic hydrolysis before reaching core fibres.

Mantle vs. Core Fibres — Clinically Important MCQ

Mantle Bundles (Peripheral Fasciculi)
  • First to contact local anesthetic
  • Exposed to highest concentration
  • Blocked first and most completely
  • Innervate more proximal areas (e.g., molars in inferior alveolar nerve block)
  • Last to recover — remain anesthetized longest
Core Bundles (Central Fasciculi)
  • Contacted by LA only after delay
  • Exposed to lower concentration (drug diluted by tissue fluids + capillary absorption)
  • Blocked later and incompletely
  • Innervate more distal areas (e.g., incisors and canines in inferior alveolar block)
  • First to recover — lose anesthesia first

Induction Time
Period from deposition of anesthetic solution to complete conduction blockade.

• Diffusion constant of the drug

Section 14

Tachyphylaxis & Recovery from Block Exam Favourite

"Define tachyphylaxis and describe its causes" — classic short-answer question.

Definition of Tachyphylaxis
Tachyphylaxis is defined as increasing tolerance to a drug that is administered repeatedly. In the context of local anesthesia, it refers to a situation where repeated injection of a local anesthetic produces diminishing anesthetic effect. Much more likely to develop if nerve function returns before reinjection (patient complains of pain).

Causes of Tachyphylaxis (6 Causes)

  • 1
    Oedema
    Isolates the nerve from contact with local anesthetic solution.
  • 2
    Localized Haemorrhage
    Blood products form a physical barrier isolating the nerve from the anesthetic.
  • 3
    Clot Formation
    Fibrin clot isolates the nerve from the anesthetic solution.
  • 4
    Transudation
    Protein-rich fluid accumulation physically isolates nerve from drug.
  • 5
    Hypernatraemia
    Raises the sodium ion gradient, thereby counteracting the decrease in sodium ion conduction brought about by the local anesthetic.
  • 6
    Decreased pH of Tissues
    Brought about by the first injection of the acidic local anesthetic solution. Ambient pH at injection site becomes lower on reinjection → fewer RN molecules are formed → less drug diffuses across nerve sheath.

Recovery from Nerve Block

Recovery Pattern
• Emergence follows the same diffusion pattern as induction but in reverse order
• Extraneural LA concentration continually depleted by diffusion, dispersion, and vascular uptake
• When intraneural concentration exceeds extraneural → LA begins to diffuse out of nerve
• Mantle fibres lose LA first → core fibres lose LA last (opposite of induction)
• Recovery is slower than induction — because LA is bound to receptor sites in sodium channels and is released more slowly than it was absorbed
• Longer-acting agents (bupivacaine, etidocaine — 94–95% protein binding) are more firmly bound → released more slowly → longer duration

Vasoactivity and Duration

High Vasodilator Activity (e.g., Procaine)
Increases vascular perfusion at injection site. LA absorbed into cardiovascular compartment rapidly → taken away from nerve → shortened duration and decreased potency.
Vasopressor Added (e.g., Epinephrine)
Decreases tissue perfusion locally → slows vascular absorption of LA → increased duration and potency. Gow-Gates block has longer duration than inferior alveolar block (decreased vascularity).

Exam Memory Points: Aδ fibres = myelinated pain fibres (fast pain). C fibres = unmyelinated (slow pain, aching). C fibre conduction = 1.2 m/s. Aα fibres = fastest at up to 120 m/s. Sodium channel density in small unmyelinated nerves = ~35/μm; at nodes of Ranvier in myelinated fibres = up to 20,000/μm.

Differential Nerve Block — Order of Blockade

Sequence of Fibre Blockade (Small → Large)
Local anesthetics block smaller fibres before larger fibres. Order of blockade:
1. Autonomic fibres (B, C sympathetic) — first blocked → vasodilation, skin warm
2. C fibres (pain, temperature) — blocked early → loss of pain/temperature sensation
3. Aδ fibres (pain, temperature, touch) — small myelinated
4. Aγ fibres (motor to muscle spindles)
5. Aβ fibres (pressure, touch)
6. Aα fibres (motor, proprioception) — last blocked, largest

Recovery occurs in the reverse order — largest fibres recover first.
